The documents cover fundamental valuation metrics (P/E ratio) and technical analysis methodologies. P/E ratios compare stock prices to earnings for valuation, with variations including trailing, forward, absolute, and relative forms. Technical analysis studies price/volume patterns to forecast trends, with roots in Dow Theory and trend identification through charts, support/resistance, and breakouts. **Trading implications:**
- P/E ratio of 10 or below may indicate value entry points with technical support convergence
- Compare P/E ratios only within same sector/industry for valid valuation
- Forward P/E lower than trailing P/E suggests expected earnings growth acceleration
- Use PEG ratio < 1 as potential undervalued growth signal
- Trailing P/E provides objective historical baseline while forward P/E reflects growth expectations
- Trend identification through peaks/troughs analysis enables directional positioning
- Breakout confirmation with volume validates trend continuation entries
- Support/resistance levels at round numbers create actionable price zones
- Combine fundamental P/E screening with technical chart support for hybrid entry strategy
- Stops (protective, trailing, time-based) essential for breakout trade risk management

**Risk factors:**
- Companies with zero/negative earnings have no meaningful P/E (N/A)
- Forward P/E relies on potentially manipulated or inaccurate earnings guidance
- Trailing P/E becomes stale between quarterly earnings releases
- Cross-industry P/E comparisons misleading due to different growth/earnings profiles
- Accounting manipulation can distort reported EPS and P/E reliability
- Debt levels affect P/E ratios through leverage, creating comparability issues
- Technical analysis efficacy disputed by efficient-market hypothesis
- Breakout failures ('specialist breakouts') require stop-loss discipline
- Market sentiment extremes may contrarian signal but timing remains uncertain
- Historical patterns may not predict future price movements
