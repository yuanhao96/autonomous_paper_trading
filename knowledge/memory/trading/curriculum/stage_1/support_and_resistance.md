## 2026-03-03 — Technical Analysis - The Complete Resource for Financial Market Technicians 2nd edition 2010 — part 3

Support and resistance levels are foundational technical analysis concepts where prices tend to stop and reverse due to supply-demand imbalances and market psychology. These levels are identified through multiple price touches without breakthrough, strengthened by trading volume and historical memory. When broken, support becomes resistance and vice versa, with applications ranging from trend identification to stop placement and breakout confirmation.

**Key concepts:** Support level: price floor where buying interest prevents further decline, Resistance level: price ceiling where selling interest halts advances, Trend identification through peaks and troughs per Dow Theory, Breakout: price movement beyond support/resistance signaling potential trend change, Reactive vs. proactive support/resistance methods, Role reversal: broken support becomes resistance, broken resistance becomes support, Psychological levels at round numbers (00, 50), Volume-price analysis validation of support/resistance strength

**Trading implications:**
- Enter long positions near confirmed support levels with stop-losses below
- Enter short positions near confirmed resistance levels with stop-losses above
- Use breakouts beyond support/resistance as entry signals with volume confirmation
- Place trailing stops to protect profits as trends develop
- Anticipate role reversal when levels are breached—former resistance becomes support for re-entry
- Wait for retracements to broken levels for lower-risk entry opportunities
- Combine with volume analysis to validate breakout authenticity versus false signals
- Use time stops to exit positions that don't move as expected within defined periods

**Risk factors:**
- False breakouts ('specialist breakouts') that trap traders before reversing
- Subjective interpretation of trend lines and level placement
- Market efficiency debates challenge predictive validity of technical patterns
- Over-reliance on single indicators without confirmation from multiple tools
- Behavioral biases causing premature entry before level confirmation
- Noise exceeding threshold can invalidate level significance
- Changing market conditions may render historical support/resistance obsolete
